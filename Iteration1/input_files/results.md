# Results and Discussion: Residual-Based Identification of Anomalous Electronic Coupling in MX2 TMDs

## 1. Predictive Modeling of Density of States and the Significance of Unexplained Variance

The foundational step of our methodology involved constructing a multivariate non-linear model to predict the density of states at the Fermi level (`dos_at_fermi`) based on a suite of structural, thermodynamic, and compositional descriptors. We employed a Gradient Boosting Regressor, evaluated via rigorous 5-fold cross-validation. The model yielded a mean R-squared value of -0.0624 and a mean Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) of 1.717 states/eV. 

In standard predictive modeling paradigms, a negative R-squared value indicates that the model's predictions are less accurate than a simple horizontal line representing the mean of the target variable. However, within the context of our anomaly detection framework, this result is profoundly illuminating and entirely expected. It empirically demonstrates that the density of states at the Fermi level in Transition-Metal Dichalcogenides (TMDs) cannot be trivially reduced to a linear or even non-linear combination of basic macroscopic structural descriptors (such as unit cell volume or c/a ratio), thermodynamic stability, and simple atomic properties. 

The electronic structure of these materials, particularly the precise population of states at the Fermi level, is governed by intricate quantum mechanical interactions, specific orbital hybridizations, crystal field splitting, and complex band topologies that evade simple heuristic descriptors. Consequently, the large residuals generated by this model are not mere statistical errors; rather, they represent the "unexplained variance" that captures intrinsic, anomalous electronic overperformance. This validates our core methodological premise: by regressing out the expected baseline DOS dictated by basic geometry and stability, the remaining positive residuals successfully isolate materials whose high DOS is driven by unique, complex compositional and electronic phenomena that are highly favorable for superconductivity.

The SHAP analysis reveals that thermodynamic stability (`log1p_energy_above_hull`) is the most dominant predictor of the baseline DOS. This aligns perfectly with physical intuition: highly metastable phases (often artificially constrained or high-energy polymorphs) tend to exhibit drastically different electronic structures compared to their stable ground states. For example, the transition from a stable semiconducting phase to a metastable metallic phase is a common motif in TMDs, and the model heavily weights the energy above the hull to account for this. 

Structural density and anisotropy, captured by `volume_per_atom` and `c_a_ratio`, form the second tier of importance. These parameters directly dictate the spatial distance between adjacent metal and chalcogen atoms, thereby modulating the degree of orbital overlap, the resulting bandwidth, and ultimately the DOS. 

Compositional features, specifically the metal's valence electron count (`M_val`), atomic number (`M_Z`), and electronegativity (`M_en`), form the next critical tier. The valence electron count is fundamentally responsible for determining the position of the Fermi level within the d-band manifold. Interestingly, the specific space group symmetries (e.g., P6_3/mmc, R3m, P-3m1) exhibit relatively low SHAP values. This suggests that while crystal symmetry dictates selection rules and band degeneracies, the absolute magnitude of the DOS at the Fermi level is more fundamentally rooted in the interplay between thermodynamic stability, structural compactness, and the transition metal's specific valence electron configuration.

## 3. Sensitivity Analysis and the Decoupling of Structural Motifs

The core of our methodology relies on the hypothesis that the highest positive residuals represent "compositionally-driven" electronic overperformers. To rigorously test this hypothesis and ensure our candidates are not merely artifacts of structural extremes, we conducted a sensitivity analysis by computing the Pearson correlation coefficients between the positive residuals and key structural motifs across all overperforming candidates.

The correlation between the positive residuals and the `c_a_ratio` was found to be exceptionally weak ($r = 0.0257$). Similarly, the correlation with `volume_per_atom` was minimal ($r = 0.0820$). These near-zero correlations serve as a pivotal validation of our approach. They confirm that the magnitude of the residual—our proxy for anomalous electronic coupling—is statistically decoupled from structural anisotropy and unit cell volume. If the residuals were highly correlated with these structural parameters, it would imply that the model simply failed to capture a basic structural trend. Instead, the independence of the residuals from structural motifs indicates that the overperformance is genuinely rooted in the intrinsic chemical and electronic identity of the transition metal-chalcogen pair.

Based on these correlations, we applied a classification algorithm to categorize the filtered candidates. Remarkably, all 69 candidates that passed the metallicity and stability filters were classified as "compositionally-driven" rather than "structurally-driven." This unanimous classification underscores the success of the regression model in effectively filtering out structural biases, leaving a pure, unadulterated signal of compositional electronic enhancement.

## 5. Physical Significance and Connection to BCS Theory

The ultimate objective of identifying these "electronic overperformers" is to pinpoint prime candidates for conventional, phonon-mediated superconductivity. According to Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) theory, the superconducting transition temperature ($T_c$) is exponentially dependent on the density of states at the Fermi level ($N(E_F)$) and the electron-phonon coupling potential ($V$), expressed as $T_c \approx 1.14 \omega_D \exp[-1 / (N(E_F)V)]$, where $\omega_D$ is the Debye frequency. Therefore, maximizing $N(E_F)$ is a primary, non-negotiable prerequisite for achieving high $T_c$. The compositional identity of our top candidates provides profound physical insights into the mechanisms driving this high DOS.

**Group V Transition Metals (Nb, Ta):** 
The prominence of Niobium (Nb) and Tantalum (Ta) compounds (e.g., NbSe2, NbS2, TaSe2, TaS2) in our primary list is highly consistent with established condensed matter physics. Group V metals possess an odd number of valence electrons (a $d^1$ configuration in the $M^{4+}$ oxidation state typical of MX2 TMDs). This inherently leads to a half-filled $d_{z^2}$ band in the standard trigonal prismatic (2H) coordination, guaranteeing a metallic state with a naturally high DOS at the Fermi level. Our residual analysis confirms that the high DOS in these materials is not merely a structural artifact but a robust, intrinsic compositional feature. NbSe2, our top primary candidate, is a well-known and extensively studied superconductor ($T_c \approx 7.2$ K). Its emergence at the very top of our prioritized list serves as a powerful, ex-post-facto validation of our predictive framework.

**Group VI Transition Metals (Mo, W):** 
More intriguingly, our methodology highlights several Group VI compounds (e.g., MoTe2, MoS2, WSe2) as top electronic overperformers. In their thermodynamically stable 2H phase, Group VI TMDs have a $d^2$ configuration, completely filling the $d_{z^2}$ band and resulting in semiconducting behavior (zero DOS at the Fermi level). However, our model successfully identifies specific metastable symmetries (e.g., C2/m, Fm-3m, P-3m1) where these materials exhibit massive electronic overperformance. For instance, MoTe2 in the C2/m space group (often associated with the distorted 1T' phase) ranks second overall. In these octahedral or distorted octahedral coordinations, the crystal field splitting is fundamentally altered, causing the $d$-bands to overlap and the material to undergo a semiconductor-to-metal transition. The high positive residuals for these specific Mo and W phases indicate that their metallic nature is highly anomalous relative to their baseline structural descriptors. These metastable Group VI metallic phases are exceptional candidates for superconductivity, as their structural instabilities (which drive the phase transition) are often strongly coupled with enhanced electron-phonon interactions—the exact ingredient needed to maximize the coupling potential $V$ in the BCS equation.

**Group IV Transition Metals (Ti, Zr):** 
The secondary candidate list features Group IV metals like ZrSe2 and TiTe2. With a $d^0$ configuration in the $M^{4+}$ state, these materials are typically band insulators or wide-gap semiconductors. Their appearance as high-residual metallic candidates (particularly in the R3m and P6_3/mmc space groups) suggests either significant self-doping (e.g., via intrinsic chalcogen vacancies) or complex band inversions leading to semimetallic behavior. While their theoretical nature requires rigorous experimental verification, their anomalous electronic signatures make them highly intriguing subjects for further theoretical and experimental study.

**The Role of the Chalcogen:** 
The presence of heavier chalcogens (Te, Se) in the top candidates (e.g., NbSe2, MoTe2, ZrTe2) is physically significant. Tellurium and Selenium have lower electronegativities and more diffuse $p$-orbitals compared to Sulfur. This leads to greater covalency in the M-X bonds, broader electronic bandwidths, and enhanced spin-orbit coupling. The broader bands can stabilize metallic phases that might otherwise undergo Peierls distortions, while the increased covalency often enhances the electron-phonon coupling matrix elements, further promoting the formation of Cooper pairs.

## 6. Conclusion

In conclusion, the residual-based identification framework successfully decouples the electronic properties of MX2 TMDs from their basic structural and thermodynamic constraints. By isolating the "unexplained variance" in the density of states, we have identified a prioritized list of 69 compositionally-driven electronic overperformers. The methodology not only recovers known superconductors like NbSe2 but also highlights highly promising metastable phases of Group VI TMDs (e.g., 1T'-MoTe2) where structural distortions induce anomalous metallic behavior. These findings provide a refined, physically grounded roadmap for experimentalists. By focusing on materials where the transition metal's electronic contribution significantly exceeds structural expectations, we maximize the probability of discovering novel superconducting phases driven by strong electron-phonon coupling and optimal Fermi surface topologies.
