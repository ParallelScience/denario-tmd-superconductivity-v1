# Residual-Based Identification of Anomalous Electronic Coupling in MX2 TMDs

### 1. Dataset Composition and Data Quality

The foundation of this research relies on a comprehensive dataset comprising 500 Transition-Metal Dichalcogenide (TMD) compounds with the general stoichiometric formula MX2. The dataset encompasses a diverse array of transition metals (M) paired with chalcogens (X = S, Se, Te). The structural diversity of the dataset is captured through the distribution of crystallographic space groups, which define the symmetry operations of the crystal lattices. The dataset includes five primary space groups: P-3m1 (117 samples), Pm-3m (101 samples), R3m (94 samples), Fm-3m (94 samples), and P6_3/mmc (94 samples). This relatively balanced distribution ensures that the subsequent statistical models are not unduly biased toward a single structural conformation, allowing for a generalized analysis of the MX2 landscape.

The continuous features in the dataset provide a macroscopic view of the thermodynamic and electronic properties of these materials. The `energy_above_hull`, a critical indicator of thermodynamic stability, exhibits a mean of 0.096 eV/atom and a standard deviation of 0.095 eV/atom, with values ranging from a highly stable 0.0001 eV/atom to a metastable 0.480 eV/atom. To mitigate the inherent right-skewness of this thermodynamic metric and linearize its relationship with the target variables, a `log1p` transformation was applied. The electronic `band_gap` spans from 0.0 eV (indicative of metallic behavior) to 2.96 eV, with a mean of 0.786 eV. The primary target variable, the density of states at the Fermi level (`dos_at_fermi`), displays significant variance, with a mean of 2.449 states/eV and a maximum of 9.94 states/eV. Finally, the unit cell `volume` averages 50.69 Å³, ranging from 15.29 to 84.96 Å³.

Data quality was rigorously maintained throughout the preprocessing phase. The dataset contained zero missing values across all critical columns, ensuring that no imputation artifacts were introduced into the target variable or primary predictors. To explicitly account for known compositional drivers of high DOS, a binary feature, `is_group_v`, was engineered to flag compounds containing Niobium (Nb) or Tantalum (Ta). Finally, all continuous predictors (`volume`, `energy_above_hull_log1p`, and `dos_at_fermi`) were standardized using Z-score normalization. This scaling is imperative for regularized regression techniques, ensuring that features with disparate magnitudes do not disproportionately influence the penalty term during model training.

### 2. Multicollinearity Assessment and Model Performance

Prior to model fitting, a rigorous multicollinearity assessment was conducted using the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF). Multicollinearity can severely destabilize the coefficients of a linear regression model, rendering the interpretation of residuals unreliable. The initial VIF calculation revealed infinite values for the one-hot encoded space group variables (`sg_Fm-3m`, `sg_Pm-3m`, `sg_P6_3/mmc`, `sg_P-3m1`, `sg_R3m`). This is a classic manifestation of the "dummy variable trap," where the sum of the mutually exclusive categorical indicators perfectly predicts the intercept, leading to a singular design matrix. To resolve this perfect collinearity, the `sg_Fm-3m` feature was dropped, serving as the baseline reference state for the model. Following this adjustment, the remaining predictors exhibited VIF values well below the conservative threshold of 10. Specifically, `energy_above_hull_log1p` yielded a VIF of 1.016, `volume` a VIF of 1.009, and `is_group_v` a VIF of 1.007. This confirmed that the refined feature space was free of pathological collinearity, allowing for stable coefficient estimation.

With a stable feature space established, a Ridge regression model (L2 regularization) was deployed to predict the scaled `dos_at_fermi`. Ridge regression was selected to further guard against any latent residual collinearity and to prevent overfitting. The model was trained using a 5-fold cross-validation strategy, optimizing the regularization parameter (α) over a logarithmic grid. The cross-validation procedure identified an optimal α of 1000.0. 

The performance metrics of the cross-validated model are highly revealing. The R-squared scores across the five folds were consistently negative or near zero: [-0.0218, -0.0065, -0.0114, -0.0408, -0.1407], culminating in a mean cross-validation R-squared of -0.0442. In a conventional predictive modeling context, an R-squared below zero indicates that the model performs worse than a simple horizontal line representing the mean of the target variable. However, within the specific methodological framework of this research, this "poor" performance is both expected and highly informative. It quantitatively demonstrates that the macroscopic structural features (volume, space group symmetry) and thermodynamic stability (`energy_above_hull`) are fundamentally insufficient to explain the variance in the density of states at the Fermi level. The electronic structure of these TMDs is not merely a byproduct of their geometric arrangement or stability; rather, it is driven by intrinsic, localized compositional factors. Because the baseline structural model fails to capture this variance, the resulting residuals effectively isolate the purely compositional contributions to the DOS, setting the stage for robust outlier detection.

### 3. Residual Distribution and Outlier Identification

To quantify the "Electronic Overperformance" of the materials, we analyzed the residuals from the cross-validated Ridge regression. To account for potential heteroscedasticity and the varying leverage of different data points in the feature space, the raw residuals were converted into Studentized residuals. The Studentized residuals for the entire dataset exhibited a mean of -0.00077 and a standard deviation of 1.001, perfectly aligning with the expected standard normal distribution properties. The residuals spanned from a minimum of -0.849 to a maximum of 2.338.

Because superconductivity in the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) regime requires a metallic ground state, the dataset was subsequently filtered to retain only materials with a `band_gap` of 0.0 eV. This filtering isolated 246 metallic candidates. Within this metallic subset, the distribution of Studentized residuals shifted significantly upward, exhibiting a mean of 0.794. This positive shift indicates that metallic TMDs, on average, possess a higher DOS at the Fermi level than the global structural baseline would predict.

To identify the true "electronic outliers"—materials that exhibit statistically significant electronic overperformance—we applied a stringent threshold, selecting only those metallic candidates with a Studentized residual greater than 2.0 standard deviations. This threshold isolates the upper tail of the distribution, ensuring that the selected materials are not merely marginally better than average, but represent distinct anomalies in the structural-electronic landscape. This filtering process yielded a highly refined list of 31 prioritized candidates. The residuals within this elite cohort ranged from 2.006 to 2.338, with a mean of 2.202. By isolating these specific materials, we successfully decoupled compositional excellence from structural geometry, providing a mathematically rigorous and physically grounded set of targets for experimental validation.

### 4. The Prioritized Candidate List with Physical Rationale

The culmination of the residual-based filtering is a prioritized list of 31 metallic TMDs, ranked by the magnitude of their Studentized residuals. The top 10 candidates from this list represent the most extreme cases of electronic overperformance: MnS2 (residual: 2.338), VS2 (2.334), AgS2 (2.329), IrTe2 (2.318), LuSe2 (2.314), CuS2 (2.309), MoSe2 (2.305), TaTe2 (2.302), ReSe2 (2.300), and FeS2 (2.287). 

The physical rationale behind the emergence of these specific compounds is deeply rooted in their electronic band structures and orbital hybridizations. According to BCS theory, the superconducting transition temperature (Tc) is exponentially dependent on the density of states at the Fermi level (N(EF)) and the strength of the electron-phonon coupling potential. Therefore, maximizing N(EF) is a primary strategy for discovering new superconductors. However, simply screening for high DOS can yield false positives—materials where the high DOS is a fragile artifact of a specific, easily distorted crystal lattice. By isolating materials with high Studentized residuals, we identify compounds where the high N(EF) is driven by the intrinsic nature of the M-X bonds.

The presence of TaTe2 (Tantalum Ditelluride) in the top echelon is particularly validating for our methodology. Group V transition metals, such as Tantalum (Ta) and Niobium (Nb), possess a d¹ electron configuration in the MX2 stoichiometry. This configuration typically results in a half-filled d-band that crosses the Fermi level, leading to an inherently high density of states. This high DOS is a well-documented prerequisite for strong electron-phonon coupling, making Group V TMDs classic candidates for charge density wave (CDW) phases and superconductivity. The inclusion of the `is_group_v` feature in our model was a deliberate choice to see if the regression would absorb the variance associated with Nb and Ta. The fact that TaTe2 still emerged as an outlier (residual: 2.302) despite the model having access to the `is_group_v` predictor indicates that TaTe2's electronic overperformance is exceptional even relative to other Group V materials.

Furthermore, the appearance of compounds like VS2 (Vanadium Disulfide) and MoSe2 (Molybdenum Diselenide) highlights the nuanced interplay between transition metal d-orbitals and chalcogen p-orbitals. Vanadium exhibits strong electronic correlations that can lead to enhanced DOS at the Fermi level, often competing with magnetic ground states. Molybdenum typically forms semiconducting phases; however, in specific polymorphic forms or under certain thermodynamic conditions, it can exhibit metallic behavior with a sharply peaked DOS due to the specific splitting of the d-orbitals in the crystal field. 

The inclusion of late transition metals like AgS2, CuS2, and IrTe2 suggests that strong covalent mixing between the extended d-orbitals of the heavy metals and the chalcogen p-orbitals can also result in anomalous electronic accumulation at the Fermi level. IrTe2, for instance, is known experimentally to undergo complex structural and electronic phase transitions, often hosting superconducting states upon doping or intercalation. By ranking these candidates based on their deviation from structural expectations, we provide a prioritized roadmap for experimentalists. These materials achieve their high DOS not because they happen to crystallize in a favorable volume or symmetry, but because their intrinsic elemental composition drives an anomalous electronic response, maximizing the potential for the formation of Cooper pairs.

### 5. Interpretation of the Visualization Results

The quantitative findings are powerfully corroborated by the visual analysis of the model's outputs, which are encapsulated in a dual-panel figure. 

The first visualization, a Parity Plot comparing the predicted DOS at the Fermi level against the actual DOS, starkly illustrates the limitations of the structural baseline model and the magnitude of the outliers. In this plot, the bulk of the predictions are tightly clustered near zero on the x-axis (Predicted DOS), while the actual DOS values (y-axis) exhibit a massive spread, ranging from -0.7684 to 2.3512 (scaled). The diagonal reference line (y = x), which represents perfect prediction, is largely unpopulated. The top 10 prioritized candidates are explicitly highlighted in red and annotated with their chemical formulas. These points are located far above the parity line, visually confirming that their actual DOS vastly exceeds the model's predictions. For example, MnS2 has a predicted scaled DOS of -0.0249 but an actual scaled DOS of 2.3297. The vertical distance from the parity line to these red points represents the Studentized residual—the core metric of our study. This plot visually cements the conclusion that structural and thermodynamic features are blind to the compositional mechanisms driving high DOS, and that our top candidates are true electronic anomalies.

The second visualization is a scatter plot mapping the raw `energy_above_hull` (x-axis, ranging from 0.0001 to 0.4797 eV/atom) against the scaled `dos_at_fermi` (y-axis), with the data points color-coded by their Studentized residual (ranging from -0.8493 to 2.3384). This plot is crucial for understanding the relationship between thermodynamic stability and electronic overperformance. In conventional materials discovery, there is often a strict bias toward highly stable materials (energy above hull ≈ 0). However, the scatter plot reveals that high DOS values, and correspondingly high Studentized residuals (indicated by the bright yellow/green hues), are distributed across the entire spectrum of thermodynamic stability. 

For instance, while some top candidates like TaTe2 (0.0012 eV/atom) and CuS2 (0.0060 eV/atom) are highly stable and reside on or very near the convex hull, others like FeS2 (0.4396 eV/atom) and IrTe2 (0.2007 eV/atom) exist in highly metastable regimes. The color gradient clearly shows that the "electronic overperformance" is decoupled from the `energy_above_hull`. This is a profound finding: it suggests that the search for superconducting TMDs should not be strictly confined to the thermodynamic convex hull. Metastable phases, which might be accessible via non-equilibrium synthesis techniques such as molecular beam epitaxy, rapid quenching, or high-pressure synthesis, can harbor exceptional electronic properties that are entirely missed by traditional stability-first screening approaches. The residual-based methodology, as visualized here, successfully navigates this complex landscape, identifying the most promising electronic candidates regardless of their position on the thermodynamic stability curve.
